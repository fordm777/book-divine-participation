You've asked how the Ante-Nicene Fathers (roughly early 2nd to early 4th century Christian writers) understood Romans 6, especially as it relates to baptism—the themes of dying and rising with Christ. Here's what the sources show:
Limited Use of Romans 6 in Early Centuries

While Romans 6 presents baptism as union with Christ in death and resurrection, this specific theological framing wasn’t prominent in most Ante-Nicene Christian writings:

    As observed in a modern scholarly overview:

        Praxis in early West (e.g. Tertullian) began to reflect the Pauline paradigm (participation in Christ’s death and resurrection) around the early 3rd century. Early 2nd-century writings—from Didache, Barnabas, Ignatius of Antioch, Clement of Rome, Hermas, Justin Martyr, and Irenaeus—do not explicitly employ Romans 6 baptismal themes.

In the Eastern regions (Syria, Egypt), baptism was more commonly conceptualized via John 3:5 (new birth) or the Jordan event, rather than Pauline language of death and resurrection.

Origen is a rare exception among Eastern Fathers, occasionally drawing on Romans 6 imagery—describing baptismal union with Christ’s death, burial, and resurrection.
Tertullian: One of the Earliest to Echo Romans 6

In the West, Tertullian (late 2nd–early 3rd century) stands out for giving early expression to the death–resurrection metaphor:

    He associates baptism with the Passover, implying a likeness to Christ’s own passion and resurrection—hinting at a Romans-6 style theology.

Other passages reinforce that:

    Baptism is understood as not the end of sin, but the beginning of a life of repentance and faith. He stresses that one must have already renounced sin and embraced repentance before baptism—implying the outward ritual corresponds to an inward death to sin. 

“Baptismal washing is a sealing of faith… We are not washed in order that we may cease sinning, but because we have ceased…” — an unmistakable echo of Romans 6’s emphasis on dying to sin and rising in new life.
Other Ante-Nicene Voices on Baptism (without explicit Romans 6 language)

Though not directly citing Romans 6, other Fathers reflect related theological nuances:

    Justin Martyr (mid 2nd century):
    Views baptism as rebirth into a life of choice and knowledge, cleansing from sins, and illumination—connecting with new birth themes and Isaiah’s imagery—but not specifically with death and resurrection. 

Hermas (c. 150 AD):
Describes baptism: “The seal then is the water: so they go down into the water dead, and they come up alive.” This language strongly resonates with Romans 6 imagery.

Clement of Alexandria (late 2nd century):
Speaks of baptism as washing away early blindness and opening admission to eternal life, and that those sinning after baptism require discipline—implying transformation but without explicitly tying to Romans 6.

Cyprian (3rd century, often counted as Ante-Nicene):
Emphasizes baptismal regeneration—the “laver of saving water” makes one born again, driven by the Spirit—but doesn’t center on Romans 6 metaphor.

Final Thoughts

    Romans 6 theology (dying and rising with Christ through baptism) was not widely or explicitly deployed in most early 2nd-century writings.

    Hermas and Tertullian are notable early voices who—Hermas more poetically and Tertullian more theologically—echo that paradigm.

    In the East, it took longer for the Romans 6 framework to surface, with Origen occasionally adopting it, and later it became more prominent in 4th-century theology.
