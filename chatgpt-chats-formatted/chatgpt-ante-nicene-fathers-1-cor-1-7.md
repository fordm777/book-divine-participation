    John Calvin’s Definition of Faith
    Calvin describes faith as "a firm and certain knowledge of God’s benevolence toward us," based on God’s promises in Christ. In his Institutes of the Christian Religion, he emphasizes that faith involves knowing and assenting to specific truths revealed in Scripture. For Calvin, faith is not merely intellectual but includes a deep, assured trust in God’s promises.     Faith and Justification by Knowledge
    Reformed theologians were deeply concerned with distinguishing “true faith” from mere belief. They wanted to ensure that faith was seen as something reliable and based on the knowledge of Scripture. In the Westminster Confession of Faith, faith is described as "accepting, receiving, and resting upon Christ alone for justification, sanctification, and eternal life, by virtue of the covenant of grace" (WCF 14.2). This definition frames faith as understanding and trusting in theological truths, particularly the promise of justification through Christ alone. The confession’s language reflects intellectual assent to Christ's role in salvation.

    Emphasis on Notitia, Assensus, and Fiducia
    Reformed theology formalized faith into three key components: notitia (knowledge), assensus (assent), and fiducia (trust). This model treats notitia (knowledge of Christ's work) as the foundation of faith, asserting that one must first know the content of the gospel. Assensus (intellectual assent) then follows, where the believer agrees with these truths as fact. Lastly, fiducia (personal trust) completes the structure by describing the believer’s reliance on Christ. Although fiducia includes personal trust, the first two components are inherently intellectual.

    Martin Luther’s Writings on Faith and Reason
    Although Luther had a nuanced view of faith, he often highlighted the role of the mind in coming to believe. In his Lectures on Romans, he describes faith as a way of knowing God and understanding His promises, a viewpoint with a strong intellectual component. Luther writes that faith is “the correct knowledge of Christ and not simply historical knowledge” (Luther’s Works, Vol. 25). This phrasing captures the Reformed concern with having a “correct” or “sound” understanding of doctrine, which requires intellectual agreement.

    Rational Assurance in Reformed Theology
    Reformed thinkers emphasize the importance of doctrinal clarity and rational assurance, often arguing that true faith must be based on correct doctrinal beliefs. This contrasts with the more experiential or relational approaches of the early Church Fathers. For example, theologian Louis Berkhof defines faith as a “positive conviction brought about by the testimony of the Holy Spirit.” This approach implies that faith has a rational basis, with intellectual conviction being a core aspect of faith’s certainty (Systematic Theology, Chapter 16).

    Preaching and Catechisms as Intellectual Formation
    The Reformers developed catechisms (such as the Heidelberg Catechism and the Westminster Shorter Catechism) to ensure that believers not only believed but also intellectually understood the truths of Scripture. These catechisms structured faith around doctrine, knowledge, and intellectual assent, asking believers to affirm correct answers to theological questions. This formal approach encouraged believers to see faith as something that involves understanding, intellectual affirmation, and doctrinal knowledge.

In summary, while Reformed theology incorporates personal trust (fiducia) as part of faith, it also heavily emphasizes notitia (knowledge) and assensus (intellectual assent), especially regarding salvation and justification. This focus contrasts with the early Christian Fathers, who more often viewed faith as a holistic relationship and commitment to God, expressed in obedience and moral transformation rather than primarily in cognitive understanding or assent.
