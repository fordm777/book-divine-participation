5. Add a short clarification about the Hebrew spelling of חָדָשׁ (*chadash*). In the original Hebrew text, the word was written without vowel points, so the same consonants (חדש) can appear in both H2318 and H2319. Some interpreters therefore argue that the covenant could mean "renewed" rather than "new."

However, Hebrew meaning is determined by **context and grammar**, not merely by the consonantal root. The context of Jeremiah 31 clearly contrasts the coming covenant with the one made at Sinai, describing it as "not according to the covenant" that came before.

More importantly, the **Septuagint translators**, working centuries before the New Testament, rendered the word with **καινός (kainos)**, which unambiguously means "new in kind," not "renewed." The New Testament authors follow the same wording.

This shows that ancient Jewish interpreters reading the Hebrew text understood the covenant to be **new**, not merely renewed.

Suggested section title:

### Paul's Logical Chain

Possible structure:

Paul's argument in Romans 7 follows a clear progression:

1. A covenant binds as long as the parties live.  
2. Death releases a person from covenant jurisdiction.  
3. Believers have died with Christ.  
4. Therefore believers are released from the old covenant.  
5. Having died, they are free to belong to another.  
6. That new union produces fruit unto God.

This sequence explains why the covenant cannot simply be renewed. Renewal would leave the original jurisdiction intact. Paul insists that the old covenant's authority ends only through death.
