5. Add a short clarification about the Hebrew spelling of חָדָשׁ (*chadash*). In the original Hebrew text, the word was written without vowel points, so the same consonants (חדש) can appear in both H2318 and H2319. Some interpreters therefore argue that the covenant could mean "renewed" rather than "new."

However, Hebrew meaning is determined by **context and grammar**, not merely by the consonantal root. The context of Jeremiah 31 clearly contrasts the coming covenant with the one made at Sinai, describing it as "not according to the covenant" that came before.

More importantly, the **Septuagint translators**, working centuries before the New Testament, rendered the word with **καινός (kainos)**, which unambiguously means "new in kind," not "renewed." The New Testament authors follow the same wording.

This shows that ancient Jewish interpreters reading the Hebrew text understood the covenant to be **new**, not merely renewed.

The innocent one entered death carrying the very life that death could not extinguish.

Death had never faced such a prisoner before.

## Judgment and the Kingdom We Choose

At this point a clarification is necessary.

The destruction of death does not mean that God becomes the destroyer of humanity.

Scripture consistently presents God as the source of life.

Jesus says:

“The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy. I came that they may have life.” (John 10:10)

Destruction belongs to the dominion of death, not to the character of God.

Throughout the New Testament, judgment is often described as the revealing of allegiance.

Those who remain in darkness remain within the kingdom of death.  
Those who come to Christ pass from death into life.

This is why the gospel repeatedly calls people to die with Christ and rise with Him.

The issue is not merely forgiveness.

The issue is **which kingdom a person belongs to**.

Christ came to free humanity from the dominion of death.

Those who refuse that deliverance remain where death reigns.

But those who enter Christ pass into the life that death cannot destroy.
