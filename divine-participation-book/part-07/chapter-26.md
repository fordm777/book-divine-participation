# Chapter 26 — The Law as the Administration of Death
*(Why Sinai Could Not Produce Life)*

<p align="center"><a href="../part-07/chapter-25.md">← Previous</a> | <a href="../part-07/chapter-27.md">Next →</a></p>

---

## The Phrase That Startles Readers

Paul makes a statement that many modern readers find surprising.

> “But if the ministration of death, written and engraven in stones, was glorious…”  
> *(2 Corinthians 3:7)*

The phrase he uses is striking: **the administration of death**.

Paul is clearly referring to the law given at Sinai, the commandments written on stone. Yet he describes this covenant not as the administration of life, but as the administration of death.

For many readers this sounds almost irreverent. Was not the law holy? Was it not given by God?

Paul himself answers this question.

> “Wherefore the law is holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good.”  
> *(Romans 7:12)*

The problem, then, was never the law itself. The problem was **what the law was given to govern**.

---

## Sinai Was Given to a People Still Under Death

When Israel arrived at Mount Sinai, they had already been delivered from Egypt. They had passed through the sea. They had seen the power of God’s deliverance.

But something had not yet changed.

The people themselves had **not yet received new life**.

The law was given to a people who were still:

- mortal  
- corruptible  
- vulnerable to sin  
- living under the dominion of death  

The law could command righteousness.

But it could not **create life**.

---

## The Law Reveals, But Cannot Transform

Paul explains this tension most clearly in Romans.

> “For what the law could not do, in that it was weak through the flesh, God sending his own Son…”  
> *(Romans 8:3)*

The weakness was not in the law.  
The weakness was **in the flesh**.

The law could describe holiness perfectly.

But it could not change the condition of those who heard it.

It could expose sin.  
It could restrain behavior.  
It could pronounce judgment.

But it could not **raise the dead**.

---

## When Life Meets Death

The commandments of Sinai were given in a context where death still ruled.

This created a paradox that Paul describes in Romans 7.

> “The commandment, which was ordained to life, I found to be unto death.”  
> *(Romans 7:10)*

The commandment itself pointed toward life.

But when life meets corruption, corruption is exposed.

The law functioned like light entering a dark room.

The light does not create the darkness.  
It simply reveals what is already there.

In the same way, the law did not create sin.  
It revealed it.

---

## Why Paul Calls It the Administration of Death

Because the law revealed sin without removing it, the result was inevitable.

Judgment.

Condemnation.

Death.

This is why Paul calls the Sinai covenant **the administration of death**.

Not because the law was evil, but because it governed a people who were still subject to death.

The law could regulate behavior.

It could establish justice.

It could preserve a people.

But it could not **produce the life that righteousness requires**.

---

## The Glory of Sinai

Paul is careful not to diminish the law.

He reminds his readers that the covenant at Sinai came with extraordinary glory.

Moses’ face shone.

The mountain trembled.

The presence of God descended.

Even the administration of death carried **glory**, because it came from God.

Yet that glory was temporary.

It pointed forward to something greater.

---

## A Greater Administration

Paul contrasts the covenant of Sinai with something new.

> “How shall not the ministration of the Spirit be rather glorious?”  
> *(2 Corinthians 3:8)*

If the administration of death came with glory, how much greater must the administration of life be?

This new administration does what Sinai could not do.

It does not merely command righteousness.

It **creates life**.

It does not merely expose sin.

It **breaks the dominion of death**.

---

## Why Sinai Could Not Produce Life

The law addressed behavior.

But the human problem runs deeper than behavior.

Scripture consistently describes humanity’s condition as **death**.

Dead men can be instructed.

Dead men can be warned.

Dead men can be commanded.

But dead men cannot produce life.

Until death itself is defeated, righteousness will remain unattainable.

This is why the gospel begins not with instruction, but with **resurrection**.

---

## The Law Prepared the Way

Although Sinai could not produce life, it played an essential role in God’s redemptive plan.

Paul explains this in Galatians.

> “Wherefore the law was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ.”  
> *(Galatians 3:24)*

The law exposed the human condition.

It revealed the depth of corruption.

It demonstrated the impossibility of righteousness through human effort.

In doing so, it prepared the way for something greater.

---

## From Command to Creation

The difference between Sinai and the new covenant is profound.

Sinai commands.

The Spirit creates.

Sinai writes commandments on stone.

The new covenant writes life into the human heart.

This is why the prophets spoke of a coming transformation.

> “A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you.”  
> *(Ezekiel 36:26)*

The problem was never a lack of instruction.

The problem was the absence of life.

---

## The End of Death's Administration

With the coming of Christ, the administration of death reaches its fulfillment.

Through His death and resurrection:

- death is confronted
- sin’s dominion is broken
- new life becomes possible

The law exposed the problem.

Christ solves it.

This does not mean the law was meaningless.

It means the law was **preparatory**.

It governed a people until the arrival of the One who could give life.

---

## What This Chapter Establishes

Sinai revealed righteousness.

But it could not produce it.

The law governed a world still ruled by death.

It could expose sin, but it could not defeat it.

Only resurrection can do that.

The next chapter will explore how the promise of **rest** pointed beyond Sinai to the end of death’s administration.
