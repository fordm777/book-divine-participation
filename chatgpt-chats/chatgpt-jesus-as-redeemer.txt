So how did the penal substitution model develop?  
ChatGPT said:

The penal substitution model of atonement developed over several centuries and became particularly prominent in the Western Christian tradition, especially through the influence of the Protestant Reformation. It arose out of a combination of theological, cultural, and legal influences in Western Europe, evolving significantly from the views held in the early church. Here’s an overview of how it developed:
1. Early Christian Views: Ransom and Christus Victor

In the early church, the predominant atonement models were the ransom theory and Christus Victor.

    Ransom Theory: This view, found in thinkers like Origen and Gregory of Nyssa, proposed that humanity had fallen under the bondage of sin and Satan. The ransom view suggested that Jesus’s death was a ransom that freed humanity from Satan’s grip, though it was not seen as a payment to God Himself.

    Christus Victor: Closely related to the ransom model, the Christus Victor view emphasized Jesus’s victory over sin, death, and the powers of evil. This view sees the cross as a cosmic triumph, a liberation of humanity from the oppressive forces of evil rather than a transaction to appease divine wrath.

Early Christian writers like Athanasius and Irenaeus viewed Jesus’s incarnation and resurrection as restorative. Through Christ, God reclaims and restores humanity to a right relationship, overcoming death and corruption.
2. Anselm’s Satisfaction Theory (11th Century)

The roots of penal substitution can be traced to Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) and his work Cur Deus Homo (“Why God Became Man”). Anselm’s model is often called the satisfaction theory of atonement.

    Satisfaction vs. Punishment: Anselm argued that humanity owed a “debt of honor” to God because of sin, which disrupted the moral order. This debt could not simply be ignored; it had to be satisfied. Since humanity could not satisfy it alone, Jesus, being both God and human, offered Himself to satisfy this debt on humanity’s behalf.

    Divine Honor: Anselm’s model was shaped by the feudal concepts of honor and justice in medieval society. It framed sin as a violation of God’s honor rather than as a criminal offense deserving punishment. Although it focused on “satisfaction,” Anselm did not emphasize punishment in the same way penal substitution would later.

3. Legal Influence and the Rise of Penal Substitution (16th Century)

The penal substitution model, as we recognize it today, fully emerged with the Protestant Reformers, especially John Calvin and later Reformed theologians.

    Shift to Legal Categories: By the time of the Reformation, Western thought was increasingly influenced by legal and forensic categories. Reformers like Calvin viewed sin as a violation of God’s moral law, deserving of punishment rather than merely a debt of honor.

    Penal Substitution: In this model, Jesus bears the punishment that humans deserve as sinners. God’s justice demands that sin be punished, and Jesus takes on this punishment as a substitute for humanity. This shifts from Anselm’s satisfaction (restoring God’s honor) to satisfying God’s justice by fulfilling the penalty required by law.

    The Wrath of God: Penal substitution emphasized that God’s wrath was directed toward sin, and only Jesus’s sacrifice could shield humanity from this wrath by taking it upon Himself. This added a new dimension to the atonement by focusing on appeasing God’s justice through punishment.

4. Theological and Cultural Factors Shaping Penal Substitution

    Legalism and Forensic Culture: By the Reformation, European society had a strong emphasis on law, justice, and punishment, influencing how atonement was framed. Theologians in this context naturally gravitated toward legal language to describe spiritual truths.

    Biblical Interpretation: Reformers prioritized a literal interpretation of passages that spoke of sin, wrath, and punishment, leading to an interpretation that emphasized the necessity of punishment for sin.

    Reaction Against Medieval Catholicism: The Reformers were also reacting against what they saw as problematic elements of medieval Catholic teaching, particularly around indulgences and the penitential system. They wanted to emphasize that salvation was a free gift from God, fully accomplished by Christ’s sacrifice alone, without human merit or payment.

5. Critiques and Alternatives to Penal Substitution

    In the centuries since the Reformation, some theologians have critiqued penal substitution for portraying God as demanding punishment to forgive. Critics argue that it risks dividing God’s justice and mercy, suggesting that God’s wrath needs to be “appeased” before He can forgive.

    Alternative Models Revived: Many theologians in recent years have revisited the early church models like Christus Victor, as well as relational or participatory models, which emphasize God’s love and forgiveness as freely given rather than requiring a satisfaction of punishment.

Summary

Penal substitution developed as a distinct model within the legal and forensic framework of the Reformation. It reframed atonement as Jesus paying the penalty required by God’s justice rather than a ransom or a victory over sin and death. While this model has been influential in Western Christianity, other traditions, such as Eastern Orthodoxy, maintain interpretations more consistent with early Christian views that emphasize liberation, restoration, and victory over spiritual oppression.
