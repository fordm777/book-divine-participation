The concept of "evil stops with the Christian" aligns with the idea of Christians bearing wrongs patiently, refusing to retaliate, and absorbing offenses rather than perpetuating cycles of evil. While there may not be a direct quote matching this phrasing, several Ante-Nicene Church Fathers wrote extensively on themes of non-retaliation, forgiveness, and overcoming evil with good. Here are some examples:
1. Tertullian (c. 160–220)

Tertullian wrote about the radical non-violence and love of enemies that Christians are called to practice:

    In his work Apology, he highlights Christians’ commitment to avoiding revenge:

        "The Christian does not repay evil for evil but is ready to endure injury." (Apology, Chapter 37)

    In De Patientia (On Patience), Tertullian reflects on the virtue of bearing wrongs:

        "Patience is the virtue that prevents anger, restrains revenge, and endures injury."

2. Justin Martyr (c. 100–165)

Justin emphasizes how Christians aim to stop cycles of evil by practicing forgiveness:

    "We who formerly delighted in war, in murder, and every kind of evil have changed our instruments of war into instruments of peace. We now pray for our enemies and try to win those who hate us by our deeds of kindness." (First Apology, Chapter 14)

3. Cyprian of Carthage (c. 200–258)

Cyprian discusses the Christian's role in transforming and ending cycles of wrongdoing:

    "We are called to bless those who curse us, to do good to those who hate us, and to pray for those who persecute us, so that the hatred of the enemy may be overcome by love." (On the Good of Patience, Chapter 15)

4. Clement of Alexandria (c. 150–215)

Clement emphasizes that Christians defeat evil by refusing to perpetuate it:

    "He who strikes back at one who has struck him creates a double injury. But he who patiently endures and forgives finds victory in peace." (Paedagogus III, Chapter 12)

Summary

While the exact phrasing "evil stops with the Christian" isn't found, the idea resonates deeply with the teachings of the Ante-Nicene Fathers. They repeatedly called Christians to break the cycle of evil through patience, forgiveness, and love, embodying the teachings of Christ in Matthew 5:39–44.
